It is no small secret that this is my favorite album of all time.

White Lighter is a project defined by scope. The lo-fi Prelude and the lamentful Caesar are under a minute long, while the closer, Post-Script, meditatively glides past 6 minutes. But the real scale is found internally - each track could easily be four or five smaller ones; the effect is that you're always on the edge of your seat, never knowing what's coming next, settling into a slow moment never long enough for it to be boring before the band knocks you out of said seat with an explosion of sound, horns and strings, Kyle Morton's expressive voice and a chorus of harmonies permeating throughout.

This is undeniably a sad album, lyrically, but it never feels like it; if the words are lamenting the brevity of life, the melody makes it feel like a triumph, like spitting in the face of fate; if the words are telling the story of a heartbreak or a death or a defeat, you don't feel the grief but instead the budding revanchism.

I mean, White Lighter has some of the best single moments in Music: The fable-like outro of Young Fathers, everything leading up to the drop on Hunger and Thirst, the punchy Wolves closer of Morton's Fork, the almost Alt-J-y end of the first verse on Dreams of Cannibalism all come to mind. God, when the overdriven guitar kicks in on that heartbreaking, furious second verse of the Lake - that's cinema. That gets me every time.

Thing is, it's impossible to talk about these songs in terms of Verses and Choruses and Bridges, they're more like rivers; the flow can be calm and gentle or breakneck, and you're just as likely to hit some rapids or go over a waterfall as you are to float gently by. These songs are expressions of faith, of belief, of love, of loss. Morton's lyrics wash you in it, they stick to you, they come back to you months later like proverbs. He sings, he talks, he yells. When he's angry, you feel it, when he's desperate, you're just as much so. The other vocalists and the brass section and the rest of the veritable orchestra he has backing him up reinforce these emotions, and the result is, despite my best efforts, indescribable.

Put on some headphones, and set aside ninety-three minutes of your life - you're gonna end up wanting to listen to it twice.

It all started with Angles.

In my opinion (and don't crucify me; they're in my top, like, 3 favorite bands,) the Strokes, at the beginning of their career, struggled to find a niche they were truly great in. That first album careens between "American Arctic Monkeys" and "Worse White Stripes," with the occasional Soma sprinkled in, and their next two efforts were pale imitations of that. But once the 2010s rolled around, they started throwing some fun synths into the mix, started getting more experimental, started branching out. And the New Abnormal is that approach, taken to its logical extreme.

What makes the Strokes so great, I feel, and what has cemented them as one of the greatest bands of all time (certainly in their genre,) is that they've never stopped evolving. Since First Impressions of Earth, every album they've put out has been better than the last. So many bands from that era have either disappeared entirely or fallen into the trap of writing the same stuff over and over, but the Strokes - it's like they spun their wheels for a few years, then decided "never again."

The New Abnormal is nothing short of a triumph, and I mean that not just in the metaphorical sense but also in the ancient Roman sense. This is a parade, a victory lap. These men have been to war and back and now find themselves at the head of 10,000 soldiers tossing gold coins to a jubilant audience. But it's never overly indulgent - There are only nine songs on this album, and each one has been honed down to its absolute essentials.

We kick things off with The Adults are Talking, maybe the catchiest and most re-listenable song of the decade. While Selfless looks like a b-side off of Comedown Machine if you squint, and Bad Decisions wouldn't be out of place alongside, say, New York City Cops, these are still quintessentially New Abnormal songs; there's something about the tinge of melancholy all these songs share, the slight distance we feel from the singer. Brooklyn Bridge to Chorus, as a great example, is one of very few pop bangers on this album, and the chorus still laments I want new friends/but they don't want me.

Then, on track 5, New Abnormal does what Comedown only ever hinted at, and takes a hard left turn to measured, introspective setpieces, really leaning into that aformentioned melancholy. And, normally, this is where the back half would be slow and plodding and completely lose me - but this is maybe the better of the two!

The New Abnormal, if it does end up being the Strokes' final album, is the perfect encapsulation of everything that made them great. It's the golden capstone atop the Great Pyramid. And when you get to the end of Ode to the Mets - holy god, what an amazing song, by the way - and Julian Casablancas is basically screaming, and the guitar becomes more of a string section, and the walls come crashing down around you, you really, truly feel like the last survivor of a shipwreck, having just borne witness to something biblically epic.

Because you have.

The Carpenter

The Avett Brothers

2012

https://open.spotify.com/album/4RclFqtNPoThUz7327xFbD?si=b2bb6667c8184982

Placeholder

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Carpenter_(album)

We're definitely at the point in the list where albums stop really working as an in-order curated experience (which goes to show just how rare that feat is) and where they more function as a collection of songs. And in doing the latter, The Carpenter gets top marks.

The reason it doesnt work as a straight listen-through is because Winter in my Heart takes the wind right out of your sails after the stellar Once and Future Carpenter and the delightfully yoinky-spronky Live and Die. If I have one gripe with this album, it is that.

But then we're back to the bangers. If for I and Love and You the Avett Brothers were at their most folsksy and contemplative, then here it's like they sat down and decided to only write slappers this time. Pretty Girl from Michigan is an absolute all-timer, and I Never Knew You does the impossible and manages to follow it up. February Seven is one of those classic Avett Brothers songs that is defintely a mote slower and so full of sentiment to teeter on the brink of saccharine, but these guys know that; what is this, amateur hour?; and it ends up being yet another banger. Add it to the list.

Through my Prayers is a bit of a skip.

Now, don't take the opening paragraph as me saying this album doesnt work as an album overall. It very much does. The connective tissue between all of these songs is this bouncy feeling, an upbeatness of a kind unique from their earlier work. There's more horns, more staccato, more roundness and fullness. It's to the point where you can sort of pick these songs out of a crowd and say "yeah, this one's from the Carpenter." Case in point, the dual waltzes of Down with the Shine and A Father's First Spring, both of which are just great.

Then it's time for Geraldine and Paul Newman vs the Demons, both of which are - say it with me now - all-time bangers, and not even just by Avett standards. The latter in particular sounds like they decided to write a Decemberists song and just knocked it out of the park, and the former is a staple on all my playlists. How is such a good song packed into a minute thirty-seven?

Then Life, which is, mercifully, actually fantastic and not another Through My Prayers, to close it out. And immediately after, The Once and Future Carpenter, then Live and Die, then Winter in my Heart...
